I’ve been thinking about what actually happens when I make this music.



Technically? I’m driving a statistical engine. I’m feeding keywords into a black box that has crunched the numbers on millions of songs to calculate which sound wave is statistically most likely to follow the previous one. It’s math. It’s cold, hard probability.



But that’s not what it feels like.



It feels like exploration. Exploring some latent space of statistical correlations looking for sounds and images are most likely to be pleasing matched with key words?



That is the technical truth—we are mining a mathematical quarry. But if that were all we were doing, the result would just be "average." It would be elevator music. It would be stock photos.



The AI is a vast, infinite library of "everything that could possibly exist." Most of the books in that library are nonsense. Some are boring. But hidden in the stacks are melodies that have never been heard, vibes that shouldn’t work but do (like an 80s banjo house track).



The statistical model wants to regress to the mean. It wants to give us the most "likely" pixel, the most "predictable" chord progression. That is safe. And boring.

What we are doing is hunting the latent space for the outliers.



Wading into that ocean of statistical average and looking for the weird, the broken, the specific. Using the machine’s hallucinations to find something that resonates with a human soul.



I think of it like this...
The Machine (The Infinite Monkeys): Is a compression of all human culture. It knows every banjo riff and every 80s synth stab. It holds them in a suspended state of potential—a "latent space." It doesn't know why a minor third feels sad; it just knows that the tokens "sad" usually correlates with a minor third.


The Human (The Curator): Is the filter. The taste. The machine spits out 100 variations of "swamp house harmonica." 99 of them are generic garbage. Curators pick the one that has grit. The one that feels like a humid night in 1986.


We aren't creating from scratch; we are navigating.



It's less like painting on a blank canvas and more like photography in a dream world. The world (latent space) always exists, but someone has to decide where to point the lens. To decide what is "Signal" and what is just "Noise."



My job isn't to write the notes anymore. My job is to be the explorer. To take a flashlight into that latent space and find the things that make me feel something. To sift through the "statistically probable" until I find the "emotionally true."



We are mining the collective subconscious of human culture, looking for new gems in the digital strata.



Is it "real" art? I don't know. But when the beat drops and the hair on my arm stands up... the feeling is real enough for me.

Artist Statement:
This work is about creation.

About getting what is inside out — before it calcifies, before it turns into noise, before it disappears under obligation and habit.

I use AI as part of this process. Not to replace artists, musicians, or effort — but to explore new ways of producing art with tools that are, frankly, miraculous. These tools don’t invent meaning on their own. They respond to direction, taste, memory, restraint, and intention. They amplify what’s already there.

This is not about shortcuts.
It’s about access.

Access to sound, texture, and momentum that once required studios, budgets, and gatekeepers. Access to finishing things that might otherwise remain fragments. Access to listening deeply and responding in real time.

The work sits somewhere between composition, curation, improvisation, and collage. It’s iterative. Some pieces arrive quickly. Others take months. What matters is not purity of method, but honesty of signal.

I’m not interested in optimisation, virality, or proving anything.
I’m interested in transmission.

If something here resonates, that’s enough.
If it doesn’t, that’s fine too.

This is an experiment — not in technology, but in permission.
Permission to create openly, imperfectly, and without waiting for validation.

The work exists because it needed to exist.
Everything else is secondary.
